Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

In Tasks 1 and 2, to generate the rays for ray tracing, we perform Monte Carlo sampling for every pizel by tracing random rays through every pixel (not through the same spot in the pixel) and averaging the illuminance for all of these rays to get the color of the corresponding pixel. These rays start from the camera origin and cast through the corresponding point in the camera space at \( z = -1 \).

In Tasks 3 and 4, we implement methods to detect nearest ray intersection with the triangle and sphere primitives. We find if the ray intersects with the primitive using various algorithms, then if it does, we update the intersection instance with the time of intersection, surface normal at the intersection, the corresponding primitive, and the surface material.

To detect and calculate triangle intersection, we implemented the MÃ¶ller Trumbore Algorithm. This algorithm calculates the barycentric coordinates of the intersection point. By expressing the intersection as a point on the ray (\( \vec{o} + t \vec{d} \)) and as barycentric coordinates using the triangle (\( w \vec{p_1} + u \vec{p_2} + v \vec{p_3} \) where \( w + u + v = 1 \implies w = 1 - u - v \)), we can create the equation \[ \begin{align} \vec{o} + t \vec{d} &= w \vec{p_1} + u \vec{p_2} + v \vec{p_3} \\ &= (1 - u - v) \vec{p_1} + u \vec{p_2} + v \vec{p_3} \\ &= \vec{p_1} + u (\vec{p_2} - \vec{p_1}) + v (\vec{p_3} - \vec{p_1}) \\ -t \vec{d} + u (\vec{p_2} - \vec{p_1}) + v (\vec{p_3} - \vec{p_1}) &= \vec{o} - \vec{p_1} \\ \begin{bmatrix} -\vec{d} & \vec{p_2} - \vec{p_1} & \vec{p_3} - \vec{p_1} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} t \\ u \\ v \end{bmatrix} &= \vec{o} - \vec{p_1} \end{align} \] Note that \( \vec{p_2} - \vec{p_1} \) and \( \vec{p_3} - \vec{p_1} \) are two of the edges of the triangle, so we denote these as \( \vec{e_1} \) and \( \vec{e_2} \), respectively. For notational ease, we define \( \vec{s} = \vec{o} - \vec{p_1} \). \[ \begin{bmatrix} -\vec{d} & \vec{e_1} & \vec{e_2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} t \\ u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = \vec{s} \] To solve this system, we can apply Cramer's rule. \( A, A_1, A_2, A_3 \) are defined to be \[ \begin{align} A &= \begin{bmatrix} -\vec{d} & \vec{e_1} & \vec{e_2} \end{bmatrix} \\ A_1 &= \begin{bmatrix} \vec{s} & \vec{e_1} & \vec{e_2} \end{bmatrix} \\ A_2 &= \begin{bmatrix} -\vec{d} & \vec{s} & \vec{e_2} \end{bmatrix} \\ A_3 &= \begin{bmatrix} -\vec{d} & \vec{e_1} & \vec{s} \end{bmatrix} \end{align} \] By Cramer's rule, the solutions for \( t, u, v \) are \[ \begin{bmatrix} t \\ u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \begin{bmatrix} \det(A_1) \\ \det(A_2) \\ \det(A_3) \end{bmatrix} \] The determinant of a \( 3 \times 3 \) matrix \( \begin{bmatrix} A & B & C \end{bmatrix} \) is \( (A \times B) \cdot C = -(A \times C) \cdot B = -(C \times B) \cdot A \), so \[ \begin{align} \det(A) &= -(-\vec{d} \times \vec{e_2}) \cdot \vec{e_1} = (\vec{d} \times \vec{e_2}) \cdot \vec{e_1} \\ \det(A_1) &= (\vec{s} \times \vec{e_1}) \cdot \vec{e_2} \\ \det(A_2) &= -(-\vec{d} \times \vec{e_2}) \cdot \vec{s} = (\vec{d} \times \vec{e_2}) \cdot \vec{s} \\ \det(A_3) &= -(\vec{s} \times \vec{e_1}) \cdot (-\vec{d}) = (\vec{s} \times \vec{e_1}) \cdot \vec{d} \end{align} \] We can see that there are two unique cross products being calculated here, so to minimize the number of calculations needed, we can define \( \vec{s_1} = \vec{d} \times \vec{e_2} \) and \( \vec{s_2} = \vec{s} \times \vec{e_1} \). So the final calculation is \[ \begin{bmatrix} t \\ u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\vec{s_1} \cdot \vec{e_1}} \begin{bmatrix} \vec{s_2} \cdot \vec{e_2} \\ \vec{s_1} \cdot \vec{s} \\ \vec{s_2} \cdot \vec{d} \end{bmatrix} \]
